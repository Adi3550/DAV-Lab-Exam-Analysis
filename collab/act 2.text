The Commissioner's Dilemma
The commissioner reports that revenue hasn't collapsed and trip counts haven't dramatically fallen — yet drivers complain, passengers behave differently, and internal teams disagree. My first task is to test whether this paradox exists in the data itself, or whether hidden data quality issues create an illusion of stability. Before I can analyze the system, I must define what counts as real.
My Definition of a "Real Trip"
A real trip is a recorded instance of commercial passenger transportation between two defined NYC taxi zones, with positive distance and positive fare, occurring within physically possible time and speed constraints, during June–August 2024.
This definition is minimal but strict: it requires movement, payment, location, and physical possibility. It does not require "typical" — I deliberately retain unusual but legitimate edge cases like airport runs and heavy traffic delays because the commissioner's concern is exactly about marginal behavior that aggregate reports hide.
Cleaning Results
I started with 9,595,279 original records. After applying nine sequential filters with documented rationale, I retained 9,164,562 trips. This is a retention rate of 95.51%, meaning I removed 430,717 trips that failed basic reality checks.
Filter-by-Filter Rationale
NULL critical fields: I required pickup time, dropoff time, distance, fare, and both location IDs. These are the minimum viable record — without them, no meaningful analysis is possible. I did not remove records with NULL tip amount because cash tips are unrecorded in TLC data. I did not remove NULL passenger count because this field is driver-entered and frequently missing. Removing trips for incomplete metadata would discard real commercial activity. Zero trips were removed here.
Impossible timestamps: Pickup must precede dropoff. This is a logical invariant, not a statistical choice. These likely represent store and forward flag trips with corrupted transmission or system clock errors. I did not swap timestamps because I cannot know which value is wrong. At 3,226 trips removed — just 0.03% of data — this is not a systematic issue.
Zero or negative distance: A trip requires movement between two points. Zero distance means no transportation occurred. These are cancelled rides, meter activations without movement, or driver fraud. This is the largest single removal at 155,354 trips or 1.62%. It matters directly: if internal reports count these as trips, then trip counts are artificially inflated.
Extreme distance above 100 miles: NYC is approximately 30 miles across. The longest common route, JFK to Newark, is about 35 miles. Even with detours, 100 miles is implausible within the TLC system. These represent GPS errors or meter malfunction. 483 trips removed.
Negative or zero fares: A commercial trip must generate positive revenue. Zero fares may represent no charge trips or voided transactions. This is the second-largest removal at 167,541 trips or 1.75%. This directly challenges stable revenue reports — if 1.75% of recorded trips show no revenue, aggregate revenue stability may include phantom transactions.
Extreme fares above $500: Standard NYC taxi rides rarely exceed $100 even with tolls and tips. The JFK flat rate is $70 plus surcharges. Amounts above $500 indicate data corruption. 93 trips removed.
Extreme duration above 3 hours: NYC trips typically run under 20 minutes. Airport runs take 45 to 90 minutes in extreme traffic. Three hours covers worst-case scenarios with buffer. These likely represent drivers who left meters running while off-duty. 6,106 trips removed.
Impossible speed above 80 mph: NYC speed limits and traffic density make sustained 80 mph average impossible. This catches GPS errors, duration errors, or trips where drivers forgot to close meters. 2,783 trips removed.
Valid location IDs 1 to 263: TLC defines exactly 263 taxi zones. Zone 264 is Unknown and ID 0 is a placeholder for missing data. Geographic analysis requires known origins and destinations. I verified this range against TLC's official taxi zone lookup file. 95,131 trips removed.
Why This Retention Rate Is Correct
A retention rate above 98% would be too lenient, keeping obvious errors. A rate below 90% would be too aggressive, removing real trips. My 95.51% sits in the balanced zone: conservative on impossibilities, lenient on extremes. This is exactly where rigorous analysis should land.
Sensitivity Analysis
I tested two alternative definitions to verify that my conclusions are robust. The lenient version removes only physically impossible values — negative time, zero distance, and negative fare — and retains about 97.5% of records. The very strict version adds minimum fare of $2.50, maximum duration of 60 minutes, and maximum distance of 50 miles, retaining about 93%. My core conclusions about system stability and driver stress survived under both alternatives, confirming that my analytical foundation is robust.
What I Kept That Others Might Remove
I kept trips between 50 and 100 miles because these could be legitimate EWR to JFK runs via highway, and my 100-mile cap is already conservative. I kept trips lasting 2 to 3 hours because drivers may wait during multi-stop passenger trips. I kept fares between $100 and $500 because these could be legitimate airport trips with tolls, tips, and surcharges. I kept very short trips below half a mile because Manhattan cross-street trips are real, and my speed filter catches the impossible ones. I kept store and forward flag trips because delayed transmission is a system issue, not a fake trip.
The commissioner's concern is about drift at the margins. Unusual trips may be the first signal of system stress. Removing them prematurely would blind the analysis.
What I Removed That Others Might Keep
Some analysts might keep zero-distance trips arguing that cancelled rides are real system activity. My response: no transportation occurred, so by definition this is not a trip. Some might keep unknown locations and analyze them as an unknown category. My response: this destroys geographic analysis, and a 0.99% loss is acceptable. Some might keep zero or negative fares arguing that refunds are real financial events. My response: the commissioner asked about the taxi ecosystem, and refunds are exits from that ecosystem, not operations within it.
The Defensible Position
I define a real trip as a recorded instance of commercial passenger transportation with verifiable movement, payment, location, and physical possibility. I removed 4.5% of records that violate these minimum requirements. I retained 95.5% that meet them, including unusual but legitimate edge cases. I tested sensitivity and my conclusions survived under stricter or looser definitions. The foundation is solid. What follows is analysis of real activity, not data artifacts.
What This Means for the Commissioner
Your internal teams reported 9.6 million trips. I found that 430,717 of those — 4.5% — fail basic reality checks. These are trips with zero distance, negative fares, impossible timestamps, or unknown locations. They do not represent real transportation.
If your teams used the raw 9.6 million in their reports, their stable metrics include phantom activity. My analysis uses the 9.16 million real trips. The difference is not large in percentage terms, but it is precisely at the margins where drift hides.
The paradox of stable revenue and unhappy drivers may begin here: if trip counts are inflated by non-revenue trips with zero fare and zero distance, then real drivers are competing for a smaller pie than the reports suggest.
Limitations and Honesty
I cannot distinguish driver fraud from data error. Both produce zero-distance or zero-fare records, and the effect on analysis is identical. I cannot verify if a real trip was pleasant or safe — my definition is operational, not experiential. I cannot know if removed trips cluster in specific zones or times, though future analysis could test this. My month range of June through August is fixed by the mathematical seed from my roll number, so I cannot claim these months represent the full year.
